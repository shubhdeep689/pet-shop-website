Pet Grooming

Professional bathing, trimming, and nail care for your beloved pets.

Bathing

Frequency: Dogs (short coat) every 6–8 weeks · long coat every 4–6 weeks · Cats only when necessary.

  1. Brush first — remove all mats before wetting; water tightens knots.
  2. Use lukewarm water — test on your wrist; avoid hot water which irritates skin.
  3. Pet-specific shampoo only — human shampoo pH damages animal skin; lather neck downward, avoid eyes and ears.
  4. Rinse until water runs clear — residue causes itching and dandruff.
  5. Dry thoroughly — towel first, then low-heat dryer kept moving; never hold in one spot.
Never bathe more than once a week — strips natural oils and causes dry, flaky skin.
Brushing & Coat Care

Choose the right tool:

  • Slicker brush — long or wavy coats
  • Bristle brush — short or smooth coats
  • Deshedding tool — double coats (Huskies, Labs)
  • Wide-tooth comb — curly coats (Poodles, Doodles)

Technique:

  1. Always brush in the direction of hair growth.
  2. Section long coats — work from tips to root to avoid ripping.
  3. Focus on problem areas: behind ears, under armpits, around collar and tail base.
  4. Spray detangler on mats, wait 30 sec, then work from the outer edge inward.

Frequency: Short coat — weekly · Long coat — daily · Double coat — 3× per week.

Nail Trimming
  1. Right clipper: Scissor-type for large breeds, guillotine-type for small dogs and cats. Keep blades sharp.
  2. Find the quick: Light nails — visible pink blood vessel. Dark nails — cut small amounts; stop when a dark dot appears at the centre.
  3. Cut at 45° — angled slightly toward the underside; never straight across.
  4. File after cutting — smooth sharp edges with a nail file or grinder.
  5. Check dewclaws — they never self-wear and will curl into the skin if neglected.
Keep styptic powder on hand. If you nick the quick, apply directly to the nail and hold 30 sec to stop bleeding.

Frequency: Dogs every 3–4 weeks · Cats every 2–3 weeks.

Ear Cleaning
  1. Inspect first — redness, foul odour or dark discharge means infection; see a vet, don't clean.
  2. Use vet-approved ear cleaner only — never water, hydrogen peroxide or cotton swabs inside the canal.
  3. Massage the base for 20–30 seconds after applying cleaner to loosen wax.
  4. Let them shake — it brings debris to the outer ear.
  5. Wipe visible area only with a cotton ball — never insert anything into the canal.
Floppy-eared breeds and dogs that swim frequently need checks every 1–2 weeks — far more prone to yeast infections.
Teeth & Dental Care
  1. Pet toothpaste only — fluoride and xylitol in human toothpaste are toxic to pets.
  2. Start with a finger — rub along the gum line first before introducing a brush.
  3. Brush outer surfaces in small circular motions — the tongue handles the inner side.
  4. Supplement with VOHC-approved dental chews to reduce plaque between brushings.

Warning signs — see your vet:

Yellow/brown tartar Persistent bad breath Red or bleeding gums Difficulty chewing Loose or missing teeth

Frequency: Brush daily (minimum 3× a week) · Vet dental check once a year.

Eye Care
  1. Daily visual check — clear, bright eyes with minimal discharge is normal; large amounts are not.
  2. Wipe from inner corner outward using a damp cotton ball; use a fresh one per eye.
  3. Tear stain treatment — use vet-approved remover daily for flat-faced breeds; trim hair that falls across the eyes.
  4. Protect during baths — apply a thin line of sterile eye lubricant along the lower eyelid before bathing.
Yellow/green discharge, squinting, pawing at the eye or cloudiness = immediate vet visit. Do not treat at home.
Vaccination Assistance

Complete vaccination guidance and health check-up services.

Why Vaccinations Matter

Vaccines protect pets from serious and life-threatening diseases. They strengthen your pet's immune system and help prevent the spread of infections to other animals.

Puppy Vaccination Schedule
  • 6–8 Weeks: DHPP (Distemper, Hepatitis, Parvovirus, Parainfluenza)
  • 10–12 Weeks: DHPP Booster
  • 12–16 Weeks: Rabies Vaccine
  • 14–16 Weeks: Final DHPP Booster
  • 1 Year: Annual Booster & Rabies
Kitten Vaccination Schedule
  • 6–8 Weeks: FVRCP Vaccine
  • 10–12 Weeks: FVRCP Booster
  • 12–16 Weeks: Rabies Vaccine
  • 14–16 Weeks: Final FVRCP Booster
  • 1 Year: Annual Booster
Before Vaccination
  1. Ensure your pet is healthy and active.
  2. Inform the veterinarian about any previous reactions.
  3. Carry your pet's vaccination record.
  4. Ask your vet if deworming is needed first.
After Vaccination Care
  1. Allow your pet to rest.
  2. Provide plenty of fresh water.
  3. Avoid intense exercise for 24 hours.
  4. Monitor for mild fever or temporary tiredness.
Contact your veterinarian immediately if your pet develops facial swelling, difficulty breathing, repeated vomiting, or collapses after vaccination.
Annual Reminder

Keep a vaccination record and schedule yearly booster shots as recommended by your veterinarian to ensure lifelong protection for your pet.
